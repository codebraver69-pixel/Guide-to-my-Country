  1. Jeita Grotto

    2. The Jeita Grotto is a magnificent natural wonder located about 18 kilometers north of Beirut, in the Nahr al-Kalb (Dog River) Valley. It consists of two interconnected limestone caves, known as the Upper Grotto and the Lower Grotto, stretching over 9 kilometers inside the mountain.

  2. Temples of Baalbek

    3. The Temples of Baalbek are a grand archaeological complex located in the Bekaa Valley, in eastern Lebanon. The site was once known as Heliopolis, meaning “City of the Sun.” It contains some of the best-preserved and largest Roman temples ever built, making it one of the most important historical landmarks in the Middle East.

  3. souk jbeil

    4. Byblos is one of the world's oldest continuously inhabited cities, located in modern-day Lebanon, with a history dating back over 7,000 years. Known for its significant role in trade, especially of cedar wood, it was a major center for the Phoenician civilization and is considered the birthplace of the alphabet. Today, it is a UNESCO World Heritage Site recognized for its extensive archaeological remains and rich history.

  4. The National Museum of beirut

    5. The National Museum of Beirut is the principal museum of archaeology in Lebanon. The collection begun after World War I, and the museum was officially opened in 1942.

  5. The coastal city of Tyre

    6. The coastal city of Tyre, located on the southern coast of Lebanon, is a major port city with ancient Phoenician, Roman, and Byzantine history. It is known for its UNESCO World Heritage archaeological sites, beautiful beaches, and its historical role as a center for maritime trade and purple dye production
